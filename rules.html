1. Player States

Downed State

A player in the Downed State can still speak but is physically incapacitated. They may remember everything that happens around them while downed.

Unconscious State

A player in the Unconscious State is unable to speak and may only communicate via /me medical status updates. Once treated, the unconscious player cannot remember the events leading to their injuries.

2. Medical Assistance & Post-Injury Roleplay

After being downed or rendered unconscious, players may send an alert for medical assistance. This request should not be used to escalate personal disputes or ongoing conflicts. Once treated, players must use the /status command to describe their injuries and adhere to the medical practitioner’s recommendations.

Injuries must be roleplayed realistically. Failure to acknowledge or portray injuries accurately may be deemed Fail RP, undermining the server’s immersive environment.

3. Permadeath & New Life Rule (NLR)

Players cannot force others to permanently kill or retire their characters. The severity and location of injuries are at the discretion of the injured player. Torturing or permanently maiming a character is prohibited without explicit OOC consent.

If a player sustains a fatal blow (e.g., a headshot), they cannot be revived by medical practitioners and must invoke the New Life Rule (NLR). This ends their current storyline, and they must start anew, either with a fresh storyline for the same character or a completely new character. This structure ensures fairness and immersive roleplay for all.

Las Islas Filipinas emphasizes a roleplay-first environment. Characters should behave as real people would, especially regarding self-preservation. While personalities may vary—some more fearless, others more cautious—violent or risky choices must still make sense in a realistic, life-threatening context.

Not Valuing Life (NVL)

Frequent vigilantism, drawing weapons without cause, or engaging in reckless, violent actions that would likely end in serious injury or death may be considered NVL. For instance, charging at an armed opponent aiming a gun at you is typically unrealistic and thus counted as NVL.

As a civilian, it's essential to react in a believable way when threatened by outlaws. In most cases, a civilian would prioritize survival over material possessions or intervening in a robbery unless there’s a significant personal reason.

Examples of Valuing Life

  • Example 1: You and a friend are alone, surrounded by six armed bandits. Refusing to comply when outnumbered is considered NVL. However, if you hold a defensible position against a smaller gang intent on killing you, standing your ground may be justified because you have some chance of survival.
  • Example 2: You witness a robbery involving two bandits and two victims. Intervening could jeopardize multiple lives. It’s more realistic to let the robbery proceed, then aid the victims afterward and inform the law.
  • Example 3: As a bandit, walking into a busy town or storming a sheriff's station to rob everyone is highly unrealistic. Law enforcement, witnesses, and other factors create severe risks. Engaging in unplanned, reckless behavior can be seen as Fail RP. Instead, consider more strategic or lower-risk opportunities consistent with your character’s motivations.

If you are uncertain whether an action might be considered a failure to value your life, consult the support bot for clarification. Always maintain realism and immersion to enhance everyone’s roleplay experience.

Random Deathmatch (RDM) refers to any deliberate aggressive action taken against another player or NPC without proper roleplay build-up or verbal interaction beforehand.

Examples of RDM

  • Randomly roping/hogtying, shooting, stabbing, or striking players.
  • Randomly roping/hogtying, shooting, stabbing, or striking NPCs (treated the same as players in terms of consequences).
  • Planning or declaring an intention to commit violence without prior roleplay or verbal warning.

Best Practices for Avoiding RDM

Create a scene: Always build up to violence with roleplay. Engage in conversation or other forms of interaction before escalating.

Allow a verbal response: Give the other character a chance to respond or prepare for what’s coming, ensuring a dynamic and immersive exchange.

Indicate imminent violence: Use visible cues or verbal warnings to show you are prepared to act (e.g., hand-on-holster stance, explicit /me actions, taking cover, etc.).

Roleplay harm with NPCs: If harming NPCs is necessary, provide proper roleplay context. Remember, actions against NPCs can have in-character consequences just as they would with players.

By following these guidelines, you’ll help maintain an immersive and engaging environment where all violence is justified and meaningful within the roleplay narrative.

In Las Islas Filipinas, shootouts are meant to be immersive, engaging, and rooted in solid roleplay. Before initiating a firefight, it’s crucial to extend the roleplay so all parties are aware that violence is imminent, allowing them to prepare and respond realistically.

1. Initiating a Shootout

Clearly signal that PvP is about to begin, giving the opposing side time to seek cover or otherwise react. Avoid immediately rushing to down opponents; the goal is to foster a dynamic narrative, not simply “win.”

Use cover strategically, fire suppressive shots, and engage vocally to enhance immersion. Execution animations should be reserved for close-quarters situations where the target is already subdued and lacks any chance to fight back (e.g., after a kidnapping).

2. Law Enforcement Interactions

If law enforcement is involved—particularly for characters wanted for serious crimes—officers will issue multiple verbal commands before escalating to lethal force. If criminals refuse to comply after several warnings (e.g., during a bank robbery escape), law enforcement may justifiably open fire.

3. Rival Gang Situations

When tension between rival gangs has built up over multiple sessions (verbal or physical conflicts), a shootout may become imminent. Still, provide some form of verbal confrontation before initiating violence. For instance, if one gang corners another with drawn weapons, it’s reasonable for a shootout to start quickly after an initial verbal exchange.

The key is ensuring everyone understands that a shootout is happening and that the escalation follows logical roleplay. Taking time to build the scene gradually avoids confusion and creates a dynamic, realistic, and enjoyable experience for all.

Meta-gaming is using knowledge, information, or details that your character could not realistically have learned through normal in-character (IC) experiences. This includes insights gained from live streams, videos, out-of-character (OOC) chats, or any other external source. Engaging in meta-gaming is strictly prohibited and may result in staff evaluation and disciplinary action.

1. IC Knowledge vs. Player Knowledge

Your character can only act on what they have seen, heard, or experienced in the game world. While you, as a player, may possess additional information, using it to gain an advantage is considered unfair and immersion-breaking.

2. Separate Storylines & Characters

Meta-gaming also covers transferring knowledge or conflicts between different characters. Each alternate character should have a fully independent storyline. For example, unresolved conflicts from a deceased character should not carry over to a new one.

Best Practices to Avoid Meta-Gaming

  • Avoid watching streams or meta chats: Do not view or participate in live streams or chats related to your active storyline, especially those involving conflicts with your character.
  • In-the-moment roleplay: While some pre-planning (e.g., for consent in torture scenes) may be necessary, extensive OOC discussion or planning is discouraged.
  • Character Background Communication: You may discuss backstories with other players, but do not share IC information (e.g., location of your character or events) outside of in-character methods.
  • Unconscious Characters: Any information “gathered” while unconscious cannot be remembered upon regaining consciousness.
  • Avoid rerolling into the same group/gang: If your character is permanently killed, do not immediately rejoin the same group or gang. Take time before reconnecting to prevent meta-gaming.
  • Keep multiple characters truly separate: If you have multiple characters, they should not share storylines or conflicts. If they do, bench one character until the conflict is resolved to avoid potential overlap or meta-gaming issues.

Power-gaming occurs when a player prioritizes personal success or “winning” at the expense of other players’ experiences, undermining the collaborative and immersive nature of roleplay.

Examples of Power-Gaming

  • Never losing a fight: Always avoiding defeat in combat, regardless of realistic or roleplay context.
  • Refusing realistic consequences of injuries: Never fully accepting unconsciousness or death when realistically downed.
  • Overuse of lassos and hogties: Constantly tying other players down to maintain control or avoid in-character repercussions.
  • Retaining perfect memory post-injury: Remembering all details after being knocked unconscious.
  • Expecting victims to forget crimes: Insisting that characters or NPCs have no recollection of wrongdoings committed against them.
  • “Jack-of-all-trades” characters: Creating overly powerful characters with no weaknesses or realistic limitations.
  • PvPing solely to “win”: Engaging in combat with the main goal of victory rather than meaningful roleplay.
  • Overuse of execution animations: Frequently performing executions, especially in PvP, which can detract from immersion.
  • Using meta-information for gain: Employing OOC knowledge to influence in-character decisions.
  • Denying opponents a chance to respond: Initiating attacks without giving others time to react or roleplay.
  • Exploiting wagon depots: Stashing or moving wagons unrealistically to bypass in-character law enforcement or skip parts of criminal activities.

By avoiding these behaviors, players can maintain an immersive, fair, and collaborative environment that benefits the entire community.

Because lassos and hogties severely restrict a player's ability to act or escape, they should be used sparingly and realistically. Abusing these mechanics can ruin immersion and diminish roleplay opportunities.

Best Practices for Using Lassos & Hogties

  • Last resort: Avoid making lassoing or hogtying your first action. Build up through roleplay before resorting to physically restraining someone.
  • No immediate breakouts: Once a player is hogtied, do not break free unless there is a significant, realistic reason to do so.

When Breaking Out May Be Appropriate

Out of shouting range: If your captor is far enough away that they realistically wouldn’t hear or see your escape attempt, it may be acceptable to try to break free.

Left unattended: If you’re left alone for a significant period, consider a break-out attempt if it makes sense in the context.

Major distractions: Distractions such as gunfire or explosions may offer a realistic window to escape.

Explicit permission: If your captor allows or instructs you in-character or via OOC channels to attempt escape, it’s permissible—provided both parties agree to maintain the scene’s immersion.

Criminal organizations can gather with any number of members, but no more than 6 members may participate directly in PvP events (firefights, melees, etc.) without staff approval.

1. Managing Group Size in PvP

  • Pre-PvP Coordination: Determine which 6 members will engage before a fight begins. Others should step back or distance themselves from the conflict.
  • Excess Members: If more than 6 are present during a confrontation, non-participants must retreat or remain clearly uninvolved to avoid confusion and maintain immersion.
  • Non-criminals & Law Enforcement: Groups larger than 6 should also clarify in advance who will be involved if violence breaks out. Civilians not actively participating in combat should quickly leave or seek cover.

2. Splitting Large Gangs

If your gang naturally exceeds 6 active members, consider forming smaller subgroups with distinct leadership, structure, and territory. While these subgroups may ally with each other, they should operate as independent entities to respect the group size limit. Avoid using alliances merely to bypass this limit.

3. Hiring Civilians

Gangs may hire civilians for non-criminal tasks, but these civilians must not participate in illegal activities unless it is explicitly part of their roleplay. They remain separate from the gang’s criminal operations.

Non-criminal civilian groups and law enforcement groups are limited to 6 armed participants in any firefight or melee. This rule ensures fairness, balance, and immersive roleplay.

1. Rules for Group Size & Combat Involvement

  • Maximum Combatants: Only 6 members from a non-criminal or law enforcement group may actively participate in combat.
  • Excess Members: If more than 6 are present, only the first 6 should engage. Additional members should refrain from fighting unless staff approval is granted.

2. Larger Groups for Non-Combat Purposes

Non-criminal civilians or law enforcement can gather in groups larger than 6 for activities like patrolling or attending events. However, if combat occurs, only the first 6 participants may join the firefight or melee without staff approval.

3. Staff Approval for Exceptions

Any scenario requiring more than 6 armed non-criminal or law enforcement members to engage in a firefight or melee must have staff approval to maintain fairness and immersion.

Best Practices for Managing Group Size

  • Pre-Combat Communication: Coordinate before a firefight begins to designate which 6 members will participate.
  • Uninvolved Members: If your group is larger than 6, non-participants should exit the combat zone or avoid engaging.
  • Avoid Overcrowding: If you regularly exceed the 6-member limit, consider breaking into smaller units to comply with the rule.

Criminal organizations and bandit groups must be rooted in compelling, immersive storylines consistent with the server’s lore. Their formation should reflect a solid understanding of criminal roleplay and a clear narrative progression. Bandits typically operate more informally on an individual level, while criminal organizations are structured groups with defined roles and goals. These two archetypes should remain distinct unless a storyline organically merges them.

1. Group Size & Membership

  • Groups must have a minimum of 6 members and a maximum of 10 members.
  • This balance helps maintain roleplay quality and avoids technical issues like members not being visible or audible in crowded areas.

2. Required In-Game Hours

Every member must have at least 100 in-game hours before forming or joining a criminal group. This ensures players possess adequate knowledge of the server’s systems, rules, and community expectations.

3. Discretion & Identity

While operating in a criminal capacity, members must practice discretion. However, they are required to identify themselves using real names and the group’s name during roleplay, reflecting common practices of the era.

4. Bounty & Law Enforcement Roleplay

  • After 3 successful robbery attempts, a bounty phase begins, allowing bounty hunters to pursue the criminals.
  • Criminals may attempt to hide in remote areas, but their real names and crimes are generally known once a bounty is placed.

5. No Legitimate Employment

Characters in criminal roles are not allowed to hold legitimate jobs or serve in lawful organizations. Any interactions with lawful entities should strictly align with the criminal narrative, ensuring authenticity and immersion.

By following these guidelines, bandit groups and criminal organizations contribute to dynamic, high-quality roleplay, shaping meaningful interactions with law enforcement and bounty hunters, and driving an evolving storyline for the entire community.

The Conflict Initiation Rule ensures fair, balanced, and immersive PvP roleplay. It outlines when and how players or groups may initiate conflict, preventing unfair advantages and overcrowded scenarios.

Key Points of the Conflict Initiation Rule

Single Conflict Initiation

  • One group at a time: Multiple groups should not initiate conflict against the same target for the same reason simultaneously.
  • Group size cap: The total number of characters (including civilians) involved on each side must not exceed 10.
  • Hiring outside help: You can hire another group to respond to a conflict, but not to initiate it on your behalf.

Allies and Conflict

  • Assisting each other: Characters may cooperate to initiate conflict so long as the 10-character limit is respected.
  • Avoid inserting into existing wars: Do not join a conflict another gang or player group is already engaged in without a clear, separate roleplay reason.

Defensive Actions

  • Self-defense: Groups may defend themselves when attacked, but must still follow the 6 armed member rule for combat scenarios.
  • Non-participating members: Any characters above the 6-armed-member limit must clear the area and not engage in the fight.

Conflict Balance

The goal is to maintain fairness and fun for all involved. Overwhelming other players with large numbers or using allies to power-game (e.g., hunting down a group unfairly) is prohibited and may result in disciplinary action.

What is NOT Allowed

  • Bolstering numbers: Aiding an allied gang or person in conflict to exceed the 10-member limit is forbidden.
  • Revenge for allies: Taking revenge on behalf of an allied group already in conflict with a third party is not allowed.
  • Inserting into existing conflicts: Intervening in a war already in progress to “piggyback” on another group's conflict is prohibited.

What IS Allowed

  • Aggression from rival gangs: If a rival gang (already in conflict with another group) attacks your gang, you may retaliate—but do not coordinate or “team up” with the other group also fighting them.
  • Defending against outside aggression: It is always permissible to defend yourself if another group initiates the fight.

Mixed Group Crimes

  • Collaboration between different groups: Criminals from separate organizations can work together on a crime if it is well-supported by RP. Constantly mixing multiple organizations, however, can cause confusion and unfair advantages.
  • Retaliation after mixed group crimes: If a mixed group performs a crime (e.g., a bank robbery), they are only responsible for the members present in the conflict. If another gang retaliates, they can only target those who participated; other members of the same organizations should not jump into the fight.

All hostage scenarios on Las Islas Filipinas must be completely organic—no out-of-character (OOC) pre-planning is allowed. Government-employed characters (such as LEOs, EMS, or other public servants) cannot be taken hostage during a heist or criminal activity, and any captive LEOs must be released before a heist starts.

Hostages must not be willing participants unless there is substantial roleplay justification. Disguised gang members, friends, or fellow gang/family members cannot be used as hostages. Demands should not exceed what is agreed upon during negotiations; excessive or unrealistic demands may result in sanctions.

While characters may trade themselves in as a hostage, it should occur only under proper roleplay reasons and not become a frequent or casual practice. These restrictions ensure that hostage situations remain fair, realistic, and immersive for everyone involved.

Demands & Restrictions in Robberies with Hostages

Demands to Hostage Rules

  • 1 Hostage = 2 Demands
  • 2 Hostages = 3 Demands
  • 3 Hostages (maximum) = 3 Demands

Valid Demands

  • Free passage or a delay of approximately 2–3 horses distance (time delays cannot be demanded).
  • A maximum of $100 per hostage.
  • Strictly 1 weapon only.

Restrictions

  • Hostages cannot be killed.
  • Hostages must be secured by at least 1–2 LEOs before moving to the next phase of roleplay.
  • No adding more hostages once the scenario has started.
  • You cannot demand that LEOs enter the establishment, dismount their horses (if already mounted), or kneel down.
  • During a horse chase, LEOs must give robbers a reasonable distance before initiating pursuit.
  • Neither robbers nor LEOs may shoot or kill horses.
  • Lassos are not allowed during horse chases.
  • In a shootout, LEOs may fire a warning shot (upward) for immersion, but they cannot shoot first unless fired upon.

Government employees must never engage in or aid any form of criminal activity. Collaborating with criminal organizations—whether directly or indirectly—is strictly prohibited. As a representative of the government, your character is expected to uphold integrity and responsibility consistent with a public service role.

Any criminal act committed by a government official will be treated as a rule violation. Government employees are held to a higher standard, and unlawful activities undermine both trust and realism within the server. Characters found committing crimes may face in-character consequences like arrest, suspension, or dismissal, fostering deeper and more immersive roleplay.

While your character may display individual traits—such as arrogance or hostility—these should not compromise the duties of a government role. Maintain a clear separation between professional obligations and personal demeanor to preserve the overall quality and realism of the roleplay experience.

Staff members reserve the right to monitor and enforce these rules. Violations may result in warnings, suspensions, or permanent bans, depending on severity, to ensure fairness and maintain a high-quality roleplay environment for everyone.

1. Verbal Engagement & Introduction

Robbers must initiate with compelling dialogue, creating a dynamic interaction that enhances immersion for everyone. During the robbery, introduce yourself by your character’s real name, reflecting the server’s lore and adding depth to ongoing narratives.

2. Emoting Fear & Killing Restrictions

Victims should emote realistic fear to maintain immersion. Robbers are generally not allowed to kill robbery victims unless a deep, well-established roleplay reason justifies it. This keeps the focus on storytelling rather than senseless violence.

3. Discretion & Witnesses

Robberies must be conducted discreetly, in places without witnesses or at times/locations that minimize public exposure (e.g., alleyways at night). This promotes realistic criminal behavior and prevents random, chaotic incidents that disrupt roleplay.

4. Group Dynamics & Value of Life

Robbery groups must consist of a minimum of 4 and a maximum of 6 criminals. All participants must follow the server’s Value of Life rule. For instance, think twice before robbing a large or heavily armed group, or targeting a government official without proper justification.

5. Rules for Robbery Loot

Robbers may only take items or money that victims willingly offer. If a weapon is visibly carried, robbers may demand it as part of the deal for the victim’s safety. Robbers cannot demand excessive amounts of money; they must accept what is offered.

Victims are encouraged to provide a reasonable amount of money or items to reward the roleplay effort, ensuring fairness and engagement on both sides.

6. Horse Protection

Once the robbery concludes, do not harm or kill the victim’s horse. The focus should remain on the player interaction, not unnecessary violence towards animals, preserving immersion and respect for all.

By adhering to these guidelines, robbery and hold-up scenarios will remain immersive, balanced, and enjoyable, fostering a respectful and realistic atmosphere for all players involved.
